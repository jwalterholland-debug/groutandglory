5 Red Flags When Hiring an Asheville Tile Contractor (And How to Avoid Costly Mistakes)

These five warning signs could save you thousands when choosing a tile contractor. Don't let a bad hire ruin your project.

You're investing serious money in tile work for your home. The last thing you want is to hire someone who disappears halfway through or leaves you with a mess that costs twice as much to fix. More than 80% of contractor complaints get resolved through the Better Business Bureau, but wouldn't you rather avoid problems altogether? Here's what to watch for when hiring an Asheville tile contractor so your project stays on track.

Red Flag #1: They Can't Show You a Valid License

In North Carolina, a general contractor must be licensed if the total project cost is valued at $40,000 or higher. Many tile projects hit this threshold quickly — especially when you factor in materials, substrate prep, and waterproofing. North Carolina routes tile work through broader flooring or floor-covering classifications, meaning legitimate contractors need proper credentials.

Ask to see their license number and verify it's active on the North Carolina Licensing Board for General Contractors website. Anyone who gets defensive about this question or claims they "don't need one for small jobs" is telling you exactly who they are. Believe them.

Even for smaller projects under $40,000, a licensed contractor shows they've met professional standards and committed to staying current with building codes. Working without a license can ruin your reputation, making it hard to win future clients — which means licensed contractors have skin in the game.

Red Flag #2: Door-to-Door Sales or High-Pressure Tactics

Quality tile contractors don't need to knock on doors. They're usually booked weeks or months out because good work speaks for itself. Anyone showing up unannounced claiming they "happened to be in the neighborhood" or pushing you to sign today for a "special deal" is running a different kind of business.

Watch for phrases like "This price is only good today" or "We can start tomorrow if you sign now." Professional contractors need time to order materials, schedule properly, and prepare. They also want to give you time to make an informed decision.

Legitimate contractors will gladly provide references, show you photos of recent work, and walk you through their process without rushing. They understand that tile work is a significant investment and want you to feel confident about moving forward.

Red Flag #3: Estimates That Are Way Too Low (Or Way Too High)

You know the saying about things being too good to be true. An estimate that's 30-40% below everyone else's isn't a great deal — it's a warning. Either they're cutting corners somewhere, don't understand the scope of work, or planning to hit you with change orders later.

On the flip side, quotes that are astronomically high might indicate someone who doesn't want the job or doesn't understand efficient processes. Good contractors price competitively but fairly. They account for proper substrate preparation, quality materials, and the time needed to do things right.

The sweet spot is usually in the middle range of your estimates. More importantly, the contractor should be able to explain their pricing clearly — what's included, what isn't, and why certain steps are necessary.

Red Flag #4: No Insurance or Bonding Documentation

Tile work involves removing flooring, cutting into walls, and working with water. Things can go wrong. A contractor without proper insurance is betting your house that they won't make mistakes. That's not a bet you want to be part of.

Ask for proof of general liability insurance and workers' compensation if they have employees. Don't just take their word for it — call the insurance company to verify the policy is current. Businesses that are BBB accredited have to meet certain standards and have to respond if customers file complaints about them, which often includes proper insurance requirements.

For larger projects, bonding provides additional protection. It guarantees project completion even if something happens to the contractor. Any professional tile contractor worth hiring will understand why you're asking for this documentation and provide it without hesitation.

Red Flag #5: Poor Communication and Vague Contracts

Communication problems during the sales process don't magically improve once work starts. They get worse. If a contractor takes days to return calls, shows up late to appointments, or can't give you straight answers about timeline and process, that's your preview of the next few months.

Professional contracts specify materials by brand and model, include detailed scope of work, outline change order procedures, and set clear timelines. Vague language like "premium tile" or "standard installation" leaves too much room for interpretation and disappointment.

The contract should also address cleanup, permit requirements, and what happens if problems arise. A contractor who tries to rush through contract details or gets annoyed by your questions isn't someone you want handling a complex tile installation.

What Good Tile Contractors Actually Look Like

Quality contractors show up on time, answer questions thoroughly, and provide detailed written estimates. They're happy to explain their process, show photos of similar projects, and connect you with recent clients. They carry proper licensing and insurance, and they don't pressure you to decide immediately.

Most importantly, they understand that tile work is both technical and artistic. They ask about your lifestyle, discuss maintenance requirements, and help you understand the long-term implications of different choices. Because when tile work is done right, you're looking at it for decades.

Frequently Asked Questions

How can I verify if a tile contractor is licensed in North Carolina?

Check the North Carolina Licensing Board for General Contractors website at nclbgc.org. You can search by license name and number to verify active status. Don't just take their word for it.

What should be included in a professional tile installation estimate?

A detailed estimate should list specific materials by brand and model, include substrate preparation, waterproofing requirements, labor costs, timeline, and cleanup. Vague descriptions are a red flag.

Is it normal for contractors to ask for large deposits upfront?

Be wary of contractors requesting more than 10-15% down or wanting full payment before starting. Legitimate contractors typically request small deposits and payment milestones tied to work completion.

How long should a tile installation project take?

Timeline depends on project size and complexity, but most bathroom tile jobs take 5-10 business days, kitchen backsplashes 2-3 days. Any contractor promising to finish a full bathroom in 2 days is likely cutting corners.

What insurance should my tile contractor carry?

At minimum, general liability insurance and workers' compensation if they have employees. Request certificates of insurance and verify they're current. Don't accept "we're covered" as an answer.
